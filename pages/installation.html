We All Scream For Ice Cream!
Installation
Shown at the CTM Transmediale Vorspiel (February 2023) and at MANIFEST:IO: The Symposium for New Media and Electronic Art (February 2023)


We All Scream For Ice Cream! is a video and sound installation by Niels Gercama and me. In Jim Jarmush' seminal film "Down By Law" three prisoners, in a momentary lapse of reason speaking in Babylonian tongues, form a bond over repeating the phrase "I scream, you scream, we all scream for ice cream!". Ice cream, its figure in advertising and screaming children running to gather around the ice cream truck produce a sense of community, all to a recognizable dinky tune. In Taiwan, this moment is reproduced in the management of waste: rather than the indiscriminate beep-beep hidden by the morning fog, Taiwanese garbage trucks ring with beautiful simple melodies, letting people know it is time to gather and dispose of their communal waste. Meanwhile, as internet platforms wage war over dominance of the access portal, we find ourselves as prisoners stuck conversing from bubbles, doomscrolling in the prison of internet noise - down by law. We all scream for ice cream! explores the ice-cream model of Taiwanese waste management and the ethereal apparition of community amidst the access portal war. As an audiovisual installation, it combines found social media footage, waste and prison material to communicate the proposed narrative.

Running time: 10’23”

iPhone, projector, laptop, speakers, speaker stands, steel, glass, silicone, human hair, plastic, waste paper

Klanksneden
Performance
Oosterkerk, Amsterdam
July 5, 2022


Klanksneden was an electroacoustic performance performed by Interior Design, an experimental electronic music duo consisting of Marcus van Esveld and me, in collaboration with The Pulse Ensemble choir and violinist Yuri Shundyak. It was performed at De Oostkerk in Amsterdam as a Ukraine Benefit Concert on the 5th of July 2022.

Compositionally, the idea behind Klanksneden was to create a dialectical narrative between the electronic sampling and synth sounds of Interior Design, and the acoustic sounds of the choir and the violin, in the context of the reverberation and resonance from the architecture of the church. The performance introduces the two elements of electronics and acoustic as harmonious yet separate, toying with subverting the expectations of what classically trained musicians would traditionally perform in these spaces, and the presence of this ominous electronic moving bass. Throughout the performance there was a blending and morphing of the electroacoustic, with strong points of harmony between the electronic soundscapes and vocal melodies, and other descents of a complete loss of tonality and coherence. Where within the context of the church, the sounds accentuate further in their potence and prominence. Culminating in a return to a particular traditionality through the vocal line, a common triumphant harmonious climactic return, with a deconstructed club-esque rhythm which loses itself in the resonance of the church. Using this narrative approach within our composition, it allowed for specific movements and overarching sonic landscape to be created, whilst still allowing for improvisation and musicality to play a central role in and between each movement.

The performance was recorded on a Zoom H4n and mastered by Interior Design.

For our setup, we used a Roland TR-8S, a Korg MS-20 Mini, a Beatsep Pro, and we both used computers using Ableton Live 11 software, all of which were being fed into a Behringer Xenyx X1622USB. Each member of the choir had a microphone which was fed through the mixer into a computer system, which were subsequentially manipulated using custom midi mapping with a Pioneer DDJ-400 on customised audio plugins.

Utopia was/is
Interior Design - EP
11 March, 2022


Utopia was/is is the debut EP from Interior Design, an experimental music duo consisting of Marcus van Esveld and me. This EP is a culmination of the initial creative and artistic explorations performed and condensed into four tracks. These tracks were all started and completed in the attic, the name given to the inactive machine room of the former NVWA (Nederlandse Voedsel en Waren Autoriteit), which is now a social housing project.

The attic was a nearly yearlong temporary project, in which we created a studio and working space within the broken and dormant machinery of the building, as a way of experimenting with how sound and the music we created was intrinsically linked to the space, opening up ideas of what spaces are, and how we utilise, visualise, and hear them. A remnant of the intent in which the building was created, and now remains largely unused. This symbiotic discovery of this location as our working space meant we were constantly uncovering new aspects of the room. Recognising the space as being vital for ventilation and electricity: an intimate insight into the organs of the building. Advocating for the potentially undervalued knowledge that listening brings.

We heavily incorporated the existence of the attic into this EP and our music. Recording the material elements of this space; the drone of machinery, the clangs of metallic objects, the beeps of electricity, and heavily sampling many of these elements throughout the songs. Throughout the process of creation we also interrogated and focus on listening as a form of knowledge production, as a form of communication and reception to the machine room, to inform the process of creation.

Retrieved Overgrowth
Commissioned Sound Piece
June, 2021


Retrieved Overgrowth was a commisioned sounscape audio piece for the exhibition Of Other Spaces: Gardens of Dreams. An exhibition organised by graduating students of the MA in Curating at the University of Essex.

The piece explores a sense of nostalgia that falls within the realm of ‘Anemoia’. Coined by John Koenig, Anemoia serves to describe the sensation of nostalgia towards an era never experienced by the individual feeling that nostalgia. The movements within the piece serve to represent control, overgrowth, and stillness within the ‘internal garden’. The piece begins with light, natural ambiance before being overtaken by a strong darker tone that uproots the previously delicate sound. As this subsides,the sound of rain returns the listener to a more neutral place, albeit grey by comparison to the beginning of the piece. Evoking the experience of hardship perceived through the mentally cultivated 'garden' space

The Squares Have Taken Over
no thing collective exhibition
Feb, 2020


The Squares Have Taken Over was an exhibition hosted under the no thing collective in De Vondelbunker, an autonomous cultural space in Amsterdam. an autonomous cultural space in Amsterdam. The event was situated around a combined visual and sonic experience - to encapsulate the dynamics of how these can develop and shape the experience of the space during the exhibition. The music program drew inspiration from this idea of arriving at 'the end of the world'. A live soundcape was created for the initial part of the exhibition, and subsequently the DJ'ing and music choice increased in tempo and chaoticness; symbolising a loss of control. The DJ’s would seamlessly transition into one another, all working together for a bigger whole, incorporating everything together for one large build-up with the inevitability of the apocalypse as the final confrontation.

The live soundscape composition was performed via an Ableton Live setup and played at the introduction of the event. Mixing pre-recorded samples and synth layers to reflect the movement of people entering the space, and their subsequent actions. Relaying a contemplative quietness while also playing with conceptions of fear and life in the presence of fear. Using the sounds of heartbeats, hospital-like machinery with synth low rumbles to create this interplay between the historical connection of this space as a bomb shelter, and the feelings of fear, life, and safety that this place would have contained. Where the rumbling of the trams which would pass above the space could be reframed as a part of the composition due to their vibrations, presence, and activation of the ceiling, walls, and architecture of the bunker.

The Score
Planned Installation
June, 2020


The Score was a planned sound installation organised by a research group on the emerging field of sound studies within the humanities. The exhibition was planned for an old unused basement within the Universiteitstheater, a building owned by the University of Amsterdam. This installation aims to steer the audience towards an awareness of the immediate situationality of their affective experience. That once one sets foot in the installation, one ceases to observe and becomes active and participatory to the experience.

 

We do this by situating the sonic impulses in multiple aural spaces; by not explicitly marking all the elements that make this installation up; by simply having the tracks which play in all the speakers be of far longer duration than most audience members will likely spend their time in this installation space. The Sonic Impulses are made up of two tracks which play in speakers in separate corners of the room. While the pockets of sound, are found in boxes with speakers in them or in headphones.

 

The Symphony of Valletta
Audio Documentary
2019


The Symphony of Valletta is an audio documentary presented in four distinct movements, made as part of Culture Lab at Amsterdam University College where we visited Valletta, the European Capital of Culture for 2018.

Inspired by the genre of the city symphony which emerged in the 1920s as an experimental style documentary; an attempt to capture the ineffable spirit and characteristics of specific cities. As my first foreray into listening as a research practise I wanted to use the city symphony film as my guide for my movement throughout the city, to recognise Valletta through its urban soundscape. This approach was necessary to critically engage with listening and sound recording, this was one of my first engagements with this type of practise, and that there exists friction between capturing the soundscape of a city and what sounds I gravitate towards as an active listener. By researching concepts of soundscapes through M Schafer and E Thompson, I wanted to actively move away from selective and elitest conceptions of 'good' and 'bad' noise. The Symphony of Valletta is very much informed by my movement, as an individual exploring notions of ethnography through sound recordings and listening.

The piece is one continious whole, presented in four movements: ocean, city, caves, finale. These movements combine soundscape recordings, soundwalks, and interviews. Inspired by the idea of the symphony, I created a narrative where i could to engage with presenting recorded sounds in the form of a composition. 'Ocean' situates the listener geographically on the edge of the city, where after a while a few minutes walk brings you from ocean calmness into the center. 'City' brings us into the historical center, riddled with the sounds of tourists, information, construction, music where moments of sonic respite can be found within this chaos. 'Caves' explores the massive underground network present beneath Valletta, whilst it is not fully publically accesible, certain underground spaces have been opened up, for tours or artwork. Much of this piece is centered around recordings from a Susan Philipsz sonic artwork, which explores the caves as being used for bomb shelters during the Second World War. To create a closer to this piece, 'finale' uses all the sounds from the previous movements in combination to create a wall of sound in which the crescendo of the piece is reached. To recognise that all these sounds in combination are Valletta.

All sounds were recorded using a Zoom H2n over the span of two weeks, and edited together using Ableton Live.
